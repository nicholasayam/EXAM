# 1 Constitutionalism

History of Constitutionalism traced by **Patterson** “ _The Evolution of Constitutionalism_ ”, which
places the doctrine of checks and balances squarely with the Ancient Greeks. Perhaps the most well-
known example of a written Constitution stems from the US Constitution, the doctrine of limited
government, the idea that a higher law controls governmental action. Most constitutional republics
tends to derive its ultimate political mandate from the polity – the electorate. The will of the people
is the source of authority and the basis of legitimate government ( **Henkin** , “ _A New Birth of
Constitutionalism_ ”). In it, he sets out the principal characteristics of constitutionalism, amongst them:
supreme law, separation of powers and checks and balances, and preservation and protection of
individual rights.

#### 1.1.1 Liberal vs non-liberal constitutionalism

- **Walker** , “ _The Idea of Nonliberal Constitutionalism_ ”: term “constitutionalism” has become
    synonymous with the rights-oriented model of liberal constitutionalism which prioritises
    individual autonomy and a “neutral” state. But author suggests that it is possible to
    conceptualise a constitutionalism not defined by liberal values while retaining the appealing
    qualities of constitutionalist government
       o Non-liberal values are typically more local than universalistic in character, except
          insofar as they are rooted in religions with universal pretensions, and views the
          individual not as the source of value but as a participant in a given system of
          meaning to which allegiance is naturally owed

### 2.2 What about international law customs?

- In **_Chan Kin Foo v City Development_** , the applicant invoked the UDHR to argue that the
    LTSA violated international law
       o Court held that our Constitution does not protect a right to property, and thus the
          applicant had to point to some reason why Art 17 UDHR (which guarantees rights
          to property) should form part of local law
       o Court observed that UDHR is not binding; for it to have legal effect in Singapore,
          it must form part of customary international law. Given that the widespread state
          practice is to allow collective sales, asserting that Art 17 of UDHR is CIL is wholly
          untenable and cannot support a valid cause of action
       o Additionally, even if right to property were CIL, it would not be self-executing, and
          must be incorporated into Singapore law. Per _Yong Vui Kong_ (2010), a right that has
          been explicitly rejected by Parliament cannot now be part of the Constitution
          without explicit legislation to the effect

In **Marcus Teo** “ _A Case for Proportionality Review in Singaporean Constitutional Adjudication_ ”
[2021] SJLS 174, the author argues that proportionality can be accepted into Singapore jurisprudence,
on 3 arguments — (1) First, as a matter of precedent, proportionality's four enquiries are already
latent in Singapore's constitutional jurisprudence. (2) Second, as a matter of principle, Singapore's
courts have the constitutional authority to adopt proportionality as a ground of constitutional review
and are not institutionally incompetent to answer its enquiries. (3) Third, on grounds of policy,
proportionality is desirable because it helps ensure the cogency and rationality of legislative or
executive acts within Singapore's burgeoning political culture of justification

#### 2.4.1 Precedent

- Rejecting proportionality appears to be instinctual, but it is clearly an unsatisfactory position.
    Proportionality review is a method of reasoning which courts can use to enforce
    constitutional rights, as it aligns with existing questions that are being asked
       o (1) whether the impugned legislative or executive act purports to further a proper
          purpose, one which the Constitution itself recognises as legitimate
       o (2) whether the impugned legislative or executive act is rationally related to, or
          suitable to achieve, its stated purpose, _ie_ would the means chosen go some way to
          achieving that purpose?
       o (3) whether the legislative or executive act is necessary, in the sense of it being the
          least restrictive means, to achieve the stated purpose _ie_ whether there is an
          alternative means the government can use to achieve the same purpose which would
          be equally effective while infringing the claimed right less
       o (4) if all three questions are answered with ‘yes’, then courts will carry out
          “balancing” in the strict sense, between the marginal addition of the act and the
          realisation of the stated purpose against the marginal injury incurred by
          infringement of the right – a normative and empirical sub-enquiry
- Proportionality review, in contrast with the categorical approach based on _GCHQ_ grounds,
    involves fact-specific teleological reasoning: it views constitutional rights and
    countervailing purposes as constitutional principles, which courts must balance on the
    particular facts of a case while keeping in mind the consequences that might eventuate
- One precedent-based argument in favour of proportionality is the idea that proportionality
    does not belong to the common law
       o But commentators like Paul Craig have disagreed with such claims as ahistorical in
          the context of UK, as early as the 16th century. The point is that proportionality
          review is not alien to English law
       o In Singapore, Jack Lee has argued that case law on fundamental rights in Singapore
          employs certain concepts and reasoning structures that are analogous to
          proportionality's four enquiries per above
       o After drawing the mirror, the author argues that all four of proportionality’s
          enquiries already exist in Singapore’s constitutional jurisprudence, and is not some
          completely alien concept to Singapore law

### 3.2 No difference in “Speech” and “Expression” in Art

In **_Tan Seng Kee v AG_** , the Court of Appeal applied a purposive approach to determine that "speech"
and "expression" in Article 14(1)(a) are indistinct. The ordinary meaning of

- " **speech** " is any form of communication expressed in the form of words, whether spoken or
    written.

#### • " expression " which can encompass any means of conveying meaning, opinions,

#### beliefs or ideas, whether with or without the use of language.

The court noted that a literal reading of "expression" could encompass any act conveying meaning
(including sexual intimacy, necrophilia, or bestiality), leading to a " **plainly absurd result** ". By
applying the noscitur a sociis principle (derived from context) and reviewing marginal notes from
the WCJ Consti Commission Report, the court concluded that "expression" serves merely an
ancillary function to qualify and describe the fundamental right of "speech" (words, spoken or
written, that convey meaning). As such, expression was not mentioned as a free-standing right and
was thus not meant to expand the scope of Art 14(1)(a).

- **_However_** , this renders the term "expression" **obsolete** , as "speech" is clearly a form of
    expression. While the SGCA applied the principle of noscitur a sociis, the SGCA in _Tan_
    _Cheng Bock_ also held that Parliament "shuns tautology and does not legislate in vain".
    Furthermore, marginal notes only serve to provide a "brief indication of the content" of the
    relevant provisions ( _Bennion on Stat Interp 7th ed_ ). It is not meant to provide the
    comprehensive summary of the provision it covers. Additionally, limiting what might count
    as speech or expression **removes the crux of Art 14 in addressing consequences** of such
    rights from an _a priori_ (earlier) position.

### 3.8 Justifications for POFMA being Constitutional

#### 3.8.1 Falsehoods are not constitutionally protected speech..........................................................

As noted by **_Milo in “Defamation and Freedom of Speech” (2008)_** “history has taught us that
falsehood frequently prevails over truth with ‘deleterious societal consequences”. In the modern
context where digital speech is exercised, "falsehoods can be rapidly disseminated in an unregulated
internet sphere" ( **_Ting Choon Meng SGCA 2017_** ), such that “it is entirely conceivable that their
swift spread online could threaten the preservation of public order by (among other things)
undermining trust in the Government and other key public institutions, even though disorder has yet
to break out physically in a public space” ( **_TOC v AG SGCA 2021_** ). There is also “no benefit in
permitting the free dissemination of false assertions of fact that destroy a person’s reputation” ( **_Roy
Ngerng_** ).

It is clear then that “free speech, or the liberty to communicate, relates to the communication of
‘information not misinformation’”. Therefore, “false speech which has been proven as a matter of
fact to be false in a court of law, can contribute little to the marketplace of ideas or to advances in
knowledge for the benefit of society as a whole.

#### 3.8.2 Falsehoods can result in public order threats

One of the 8 grounds of derogation from free speech in Art 14(2)(a) is that of “public order” and this
gives Parliament constitutional basis for legislation restricting the freedom of speech. [Public Order
read broadly]. Though it might be argued that virtual falsehoods cause no actual public order
disruptions (to “communal tranquillity” or the “even tempo of the life of the community”) ( **_TOC v
AG, SDP v AG_** ) this argument holds no weight. This is because the derogation clauses in Art 14 are
**phrased in a more open textured manner** (“in the interest of”, rather than “in the maintenance
of”). “Interest” connotes that as long as there is a “nexus” ( **_Chee Siok Chin_** ) between the restriction
and the public goal/outcome identified that the court will give effect to it, whereas “maintenance”
entails something more than a nexus, a relationship that would ensure that the interest is actually
upheld, and therefore may demand considerations of reasonableness or proportionality. In Chee Siok
Chin the court noted that the wording “in the interest of ... public order” should not be confined to
“the maintenance of public order and therefore affords Parliament a “much wider legislative remit”
to not only enact laws for the ‘immediate or direct maintenance of public order” but also extends to
laws that strike **pre-emptively** ( **_Tan, Teng “Fake news, Free Speech” (2020)_** ), allowing the
government to take a **prophylactic approach in the maintenance of public order**. Ostensibly
POFMA would fall under the Government’s wide remit to legislate in the broad interests of
safeguarding Singapore from falsehoods that “threaten the preservation of public order by (among
other things) undermining trust in the Government and other key public institutions, even though
disorder has yet to break out physically in a public space” ( **_TOC v AG and SDP v AG at [99]_** ). The
low cost of the internet in facilitating political organisation and lobbying as well as the spread of
information may allow small groups whether belonging to sensible or noxious groups to enjoy an
asymmetrical amplification of their views by dint of volume not merit and result in public order
concerns. Therefore POFMA’s regulation of online falsehoods is entirely within the ambit of the
derogations allowed in Art 14(2)(a) and falls within the category of a pre-emptive regulation against

actual physical disorder, preventing the escalation of online incitement to actual physical disorder in
a public space.

#### 3.9.4 Efficacy of Part 3 Directions

However, there are questions as to the efficacy of a Part 3 Corrective Direction in particular, if a
large segment of the population has already been affected by the falsehood. This is because a Part 3
Correction Direction only requires the one person who communicated the subject statement, to
communicate a statement that the subject statement is false. As mentioned, "falsehoods can be

statement by the subject statement maker in correcting his statement. In this sense, although a

### 4.4 MRHA

#### 4.4.1 What is MRHA?

While MRHA is a form of **hard law** in form, the MRHA also contains elements of soft law that is
persuasive in its orientation. While religious harmony is used as a controlling legal principle to
empower the government to issue pre-emptive restraining orders, in practice, no restraining order
has been issued by the government. Hence, the MRHA is not strictly enforced and is preventive in
nature.

**Purpose** : The original rationale of the MHRA was ostensibly to “prevent the mixing of religion and
politics”, and "to prevent religious leaders from using their offices as a platform for mounting
political agitation and making inflammatory political speeches" ( **_TT4 at [18.106]_** ); it was pre-
emptive in nature ( _Goh Chok Tong, Parliamentary Debates 1990_ ). It was motivated by instances of
aggressive proselytization that had made people from other religions uncomfortable and offended.

**2019 Amendments** : The 2019 amendments were motivated by the following changes: (1) **rising
religiosity** ; (2) **increasing violence** from conflicts fuelled by religious hate speech; (3)
**Internet/social media** ; and (4) **foreign interference**. These created the risk of religion being
exploited by bad actors and being used to mobilise hatred and violence, destroying social cohesion.

- In Yong CJ's reasoning in **_Colin Chan_** _,_ it is concerning that he elevated of national service

### 5.1 What is the idea of “equality”, and is it a good thing?

As a preliminary, in **_Lim Meng Suang_** [2015], the SGCA acknowledged that the very concept of
equality is **'thorny'** , because it is desirable in theory, but **struggles with the factual reality** that
inequality is an 'inevitable part of daily life'. It is therefore **unclear** how the law is to ensure a basic
level of equality applying the principle of equality generally, and to determine which situations such
a level of equality should be legally mandated in.

**[View: Equality Not Relevant]** **_Peter Westen_** , “ _The Empty Idea of Equality_ ” (1982) agues that
equality is an empty, entirely formal concept with no substantive moral content of its own, that does
not aid in analysis of legal problems. 3 main reasons:

- [ **Relative** ] Firstly, equality is about **relative deprivation** , in the sense that the deprivation
    of something is assessed in relation to something else. Hence, equity is comparative in nature
    and does not have any substance to itself. Hence to say that “two people are equal and
    entitled to be treated equally is to say that they both satisfy the criteria of a governing rule
    of treatment” but yet equality simpliciter says “nothing at all about the content or wisdom
    of the governing rule”
- [ **Tautologous** ] Secondly, equality is about treating alike things alike, but defining 'people
    who are alike' and **'like treatment'** necessarily requires a moral standard, as no two people
    are really alike. This also results in equality being entirely circular – people who are alike
    are people who should be treated alike. Without moral standards, 'equality remains
    meaningless'.
- [ **Derivative** ] Lastly, equality is **derivative of rights**. Equality analysis logically collapses
    into rights analysis (see _Carey v Brown_ ), hence any analysis of legal problems in terms of
    equality is redundant.


```
Page 46 of 97
```
**Possible Counters for Equality:**

1. Ensures some **degree of consistency** in the concept of “like should be treated as like” and
    hence places the burden of proof on those wishing to depart from the rule.
2. By requiring the government to apply laws equally, we can **prevent the arbitrary exercise**
    **of discretion** which can render equality meaningless (SoP).
3. **_Even if_** equity has no clear substantive content, there is formal equality, which can be
    determined by the **reasonable classification test** (RCT) in the context of Singapore. RCT
    supplies the governing rule (intelligible differentia + rational relation) that gives equality its
    content. Albeit the **threshold is low** since no inquiry into substantive content.

### 5.2 What is the scope of article 12(1)? How courts interpreted the two clauses?

12.—(1) **All persons** are **equal before the law** and entitled to the **equal protection of the law**.

#### 5.2.1 View 1: No difference per Lim Meng Suang

Notably, the SGCA in **_Lim Meng Suang_** held that the two limbs of Art 12(1) are no more than
**declaratory statements of principles** that offer no specific legal criteria to guide the courts in
assessing constitutional validity. Consequently, the SGCA held that any statute must nevertheless
pass (threshold) the ‘reasonable classification’ test before it can be found consistent with Art 12.
This effectively treats both limbs as a unified standard. Even in deference, the SGCA rejected the
test of illegitimacy directed at the object of the impugned legislation, in that courts are separate from
the legislature; and without mandate to amend laws, **courts cannot usurp legislative functions** or
become a “mini-legislatures”.

**_That said_** , a counterargument emerges from **_Tan Seng Kee_** : determining the constitutionality of
legislative and executive acts is clearly and exclusively within the judicial purview. Where
legislation unduly encroaches upon constitutionally protected rights, courts, as **guardians of
fundamental liberties** , should retain the power to scrutinise and strike down such legislation. This
reflects a functionalist conception of the **separation of powers** , in which judicial review operates as
a meaningful check on the other branches of government.

[ **Extra** ] Indeed, the court's proper function is confined to statutory interpretation and the
development of common law and equitable principles. Permitting courts to strike down legislation
on the basis of an illegitimate object would, absent clear legal standards, cross that boundary.

#### 5.2.2 View 2: Chan Sek Keong → Limb 2 applies if Limb 1 applies.

Further critics on _Lim Meng Suang_ ’s holding on Art 12(1)’s RC test can be evaluated on **Chan Sek
Keong** ’s 2019 “ _Roads Not Taken_ ” paper. Chan observes that the RCT was adopted by Singapore
courts largely by following Malaysian precedent, which in turn adopted Indian decisions interpreting
Art 14 of the Indian Constitution. Art 14 is, however, framed in negative terms, which is structurally
different from Art 12(1) of the Singapore Constitution. This genealogy was adopted without
adequate consideration of those structural differences.


```
Page 47 of 97
```
On this basis, Chan argues that Art 12(1), though declaratory in form, **must be given effect as a
substantive right** rather than an aspirational ideal. The RCT, he submits, was designed for the
second limb's equal protection inquiry and is ill-suited to give full effect to the first limb's right to
equality before the law. The absence of concrete legal principles in Art 12(1) does not render the
right unenforceable however; rather, **it reflects a constitutional delegation to the courts** to
formulate the applicable standards. This aligns with the functionalist conception of the separation of
powers: it is the courts' duty to interpret the Constitution and develop legal principles that give
meaningful content to constitutional rights, including as a safeguard against undue encroachment by
the legislature and executive.

[ **Extra** ] Chan draws a structural distinction between the two limbs:

- The **first limb** confers a first-order constitutional right to equality before the law, enjoyed
    by all persons as against the state.
- The **second limb** confers a second-order entitlement to equal protection, which is
    **contingent on the existence of legislation that grants rights or imposes liabilities**
    **differentially**. The question it raises is whether the state is justified in enacting a law that
    treats people unequally, with reference to whether a legitimate state interest is advanced by
    that differentiation.

### 5.4 Reasonable Classification Test: 2 Competing Approaches (Marcus Teo)

**Given that the SGCA in Tan Seng Kee at [329] left it open as to which approach ought to be
preferred, it is crucial that we determine how the approaches in RCT.**

In **Marcus Teo** “ _Refining Reasonable Classification_ ” [2023], the author sets out 3 aims: (1) courts
must identify legislative purposes only from extrinsic materials when applying the test, to avoid
circularity in its application; (2) when assessing the relation between differentiation and purpose,
courts must require proof of the existence, and sometimes the sufficiency, of practical reasons that
excuse imperfect differentiation and; (3) applicants should only bear the burden of showing that laws
or decisions imperfectly differentiate, before the burden shifts to the Government to justify them.
Importantly, author seeks to explain how the RCT should be structured to apply to both legislative
and executive acts.

Author notes two approaches to the RCT, the version in _Taw Cheng Kong_ and _Lim Meng Suang_ , and
the version in _Syed Suhail_ and _Tan Seng Kee_. The two approaches differ on three issues: (i) how
purposes should be identified at the **Purpose Identification Stage** ; (ii) how the relationship between
purpose and differentiation should be assessed at the **Relation Evaluation Stage** ; and (iii) **where
the burden of proof should lie:**

### 5.5 Protection of minority interests

What is the best way to protect minority interests?

- The **Wee Commission Report 1966** published its recommendations on this issue:
    o In a democratic society, it is futile, impractical and dangerous for minorities to find
       security in so-called minority rights, as that simply means rights different from those

    o Apart from the futility of trying to secure for themselves minority rights, there is

### 6.2 Fundamental Rules of Natural Justice (FRNJ)

#### 6.2.1 FRNJ for Art 9(1); Not Art 12(1)

**FRNJ for Art 9(1):**

- If the mere act of Parliament passing laws is sufficient to justify the deprivation of these
    fundamental liberties, the constitutional guarantee of life and personal liberty would offer
    very little assurance [ **_Tan Eng Hong_** at 30]. _However_ , “save in accordance with law” in Art
    9(1) would entail a connection to the fundamental rules of natural justice (FRNJ). As
    expounded by **Prof Thio** in _Treatise_ , FRNJ “relates largely to the conduct of a fair trial”.

**No FRNJ for Art 12(1):**

- Unlike Art 9(1), adherence to “law” in Art 12(1) is to the protection of equality for persons
    and does not depend on the validity of a law. _Hence_ , "law" in Art 12(1) **does not** have
    normative content that is independent from the substantive right accorded under the phrases
    "equal before the law" and "equal protection of the law". Hence, it is incorrect to import
    "law" in Art 12(1) a requirement that "law" in question must be in accordance with FRNJ.

### 6.3 Article 9(3) Right to Counsel

Art 9(3) – Where a person is arrested, he **shall be informed as soon as may be** of the grounds of
his arrest and **shall be allowed to consult and be defended** by a legal practitioner of his choice.

#### 6.3.1 Nature of this constitutional protection?

**(a) Right to be informed of grounds of arrest as soon as may be**

- This is necessary in order to enable the preparation of a defence, which entails or to seek
    habeas corpus to challenge an allegedly unlawful detention.
       o This means that the offender should be **given sufficient details** to know what
          offence he allegedly committed. If he was not so informed, the arrested person
          should be entitled to be released ( _Madhu Lima v State_ ).
       o The arrested person should be **given such information "as soon as may be"** , which
          means not immediately but when reasonably practicable and certainly before he is
          brought **before the Magistrate, that is, within 48 hours** , where a complaint may
          be made, if any, against with respect to the arrest.

**(b) Right to legal counsel**

- **_Treatise_** : The importance of having access to a lawyer provides some degree of transparency
    in ensuring that a detained person is **accorded due process** and not kept in incommunicado
    detention and abused, whether by physical or mental maltreatment.
- The right may be breached where, for example, an accused is able to **show the police acted**
    **in bad faith** to prevent him from exercising his Art 9(3) right.
- Provides “checks and balances required in the adversarial process” – ensures the robustness
    of proceedings and evidence: ( _PP v Jayasangar_ )

#### 7.4.2 Rule against Bias

As a subset of natural justice, decision-makers must not only act impartially but must also appear to
act impartially so that public confidence in the integrity of decision-making is maintained ( **_BOI v
BOJ_** ). This is the rule against **Actual** and **Apparent** bias.

**Actual Bias (V. High Threshold)**

Actual bias arises where the decision-maker is **subjectively prejudiced** against a party, or, in another
word, has **pre-judged the case**. The threshold for proving actual bias is extremely high because it
requires evidence of a decision-maker’s subjective state of mind. Nonetheless, where such direct
evidence is available, the decision must be set aside without the need for further inquiry into
appearance or perception.

- In **_Sim Yong Teng_** , the SGCA found direct evidence of actual bias where members of the
    new management committee had **expressed in writing their support for a prior decision**
    to suspend the applicant, **before even hearing the matter afresh**. The court held that such
    pre-judgement of the issues, made prior to hearing evidence or arguments, constituted actual
    bias.

**Apparent Bias**

Absence actual bias, a decision may still be set aside for apparent bias if **a reasonable and fair-
minded observer, knowing all the relevant facts** , would have a reasonable suspicion that the
decision-maker might not have acted impartially ( **_Jeyaretnam; BOI v BOJ_** ). – Reasonable Suspicion
test.

- In **_Pek Lian Guan_** [2026] SGHC, CJ Menon held **extensive judicial copying** is not
    automatically unlawful, but it raises concerns about natural justice because it may suggest
    the judge failed to apply an independent mind to the materials or that the losing party was
    denied a fair hearing – which can undermine public confidence on the judiciary.
       o Fundamental biases per **_Swire Shipping_** includes the judicial officer copying one
          party’s submissions **without dealing with the other party’s key arguments** ,
          evidence of prejudgment, _rather than_ mere mistakes in reasoning that can be
          corrected on appeal.

#### 7.4.3 Rule of Necessity (Defence for decision-maker)

The decision-maker, however, may invoke the rule of necessity to proceed despite the risk of bias,
because no alternative competent tribunal or forum exists ( **_Sim Yong Teng; Lawrence Khong_** ).
Necessity rule is **limited** : it does not apply to cases of actual bias, and it **does not apply to non-
statutory bodies**.

The principle operates in two scenarios:
(a) where no other person or tribunal is available to decide the matter; or
(b) where a quorum cannot be formed without the participation of members who would
otherwise be disqualified for bias.
**However** , even where the necessity _prima facie_ applies, the decision-maker must take all reasonable
steps to minimise doubts about impartiality ( **_Lawrence Khong_** ).

- In **_Lawrence Khong_** , the SGHC held that where alternative options existed, such as
    rescheduling the disciplinary meeting, co-opting neutral members or delegating disciplinary
    powers to sub-committee, the principle of necessity **did not apply**.
       o The tribunal’s failure to exhaust these alternatives meant the decision was tainted
          by apparent bias and breached natural justice.

### 7.5 Executive Rule Making

The current case may be subject to judicial review **if the executive body acted unlawfully in
exercising its rule-making functions**.

#### 7.5.1 Formal Rulemaking (through subsidiary legislation)

- For subsidiary legislation to be validly made, they must:
    o Under **_s23 of the Interpretation Act_** , subsidiary legislation must be **published in**
       **the Gazette** to have legal effect unless otherwise expressly provided.
    o Not contain differentiating measures that have the practical effect of disadvantaging
       persons or groups of people along racial or religious lines
    o Presented to the Clerk of Parliament
- Thus, in **_Cheong Seok Leng_** , appellant charged with AWOL under Vigilante Corps Act
    argued that he was **not AWOL as he did not know the law** , since it was **not published.**
       o SGHC held that the assignment of additional duties to the Vigilante Corps
          constituted subsidiary legislation because it determined the content of the Act as a
          rule of conduct.
       o Although the assignment was within the Minister’s power, it was **invalid** because it
          had not been published in the Gazette, in breach of s23 of the Interpretation Act.
             ▪ The Court emphasised that **publication is a condition precedent to the**
                **validity of subsidiary legislation** , reflecting the need to ensure
                transparency and public access to the law.

#### 7.5.2 Informal rulemaking (through administrative policies)

Administrative bodies exercising statutory discretion are entitled to adopt informal policies or
guidelines to structure their decision-making, but **such policies must comply with established legal
principles** to avoid unlawful fettering discretion or acting ultra vires.

**Not Wednesbury unreasonable**

The policy **must not be irrational or so absurd** that no reasonable authority could have adopted it.
Courts will not interfere merely because they would have preferred a different policy.

- In **_Lines International Holding_** the SGHC upheld the PSA’s policy limiting berthing rights
    for cruise-to-nowhere ships to 30%, finding that it served legitimate port development
    objectives under the PSA Act and was not irrational. The court emphasised that even policies

Fairness requires that policies which materially affect rights or opportunities **must be

### 7.6 Remedies

specific terms, effectively compelling a public authority to act in a particular way.

**Guiding factors to determine whether such a specific mandatory order is justified (** **_CBB_** **)** :

- **Availability of objective evidence** : Is there public domain evidence bearing on the

- **Decision-maker's conduct** : Has the decision-maker shown an intention not to reconsider,

- **Absence of alternative ways of performing the duty** : By statutory construction, was

    all pointed one way.)

the decision-making authority with the designated executive body. A Mandatory Order in _specific
terms_ effectively usurps that authority and renders the court the primary decision-maker. This is only
acceptable in the _rarest_ of cases where every relevant consideration has been assessed and there is
only one legally reasonable outcome.
